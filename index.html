The Flying Spaghetti Monster Lens

This project is about interpreting multiple-image lens systems, and then modelling them, based on an idea put forward by Roger Blandford and Ramesh Narayan in 1986. At the time, only a handful of lenses were known, so their idea was very prescient.

The Blandford-Narayan classification system

From Fermat's principle, light takes paths that make the travel time a minimum, maximum or saddle point. The Blandford-Narayan classification involves identifying these on the sky. Now, topographically, there are only so many ways of arranging minima, maxima and saddle points. So, classification involves thinking through a limited number of possibilities.

Note that BN86 is a physical classification system, with no ambiguities. For any multiple-image system, there is a correct answer, which nature has chosen. Our job is to work out that answer.

To see how it works, let's first consider an ordinary topographic example. As a first approximation, let is picture the Irchel campus as a kind of large-scale bowl. But within the bowl, there is structure. To the west, there is a double pond, which is a pair of local minima. The pathway in between has a saddle point. The tallest building on campus is naturally a maximum, but only a local maximum, as the surrounding hills are far higher. From geometry, there must be another saddle point.


Schematic topography of the Irchel campus

The schematic here shows the extremal points: maximum in yellow, minima in red, and saddle points in magenta. The white curves are equal-elevation contours joining the saddle points, the saddle point contours. (The small white dots no physical significance, they are for the user interface of an interactive tool.)

The saddle-point contours are special, and they always have one of the two topological forms shown here. All other equal-elevation contours are simple loops. The saddle-point contours also automatically identify the maxima and minima. The two type of white curves are the essense of the BN86 scheme. Basically, once you can sketch them, you are done.

In gravitational lenses, the yellow points correspond to mass clumps, which are naturally maxima of the light-travel time. One rarely sees images there, because galaxy light from the lens itself nearly always drowns out the lensed image. The red and magenta dots are the lensed images.

Point-like sources (that is quasars) produce the cleanest lensed systems. But most lensed sources are extended. Hence the lensed imaged are spread out into arcs. For very large sources, one has to think of them as multi-component sources, each with its own image system.
